Nikola Tesla - the Father of Electricity

By Aditya Gupta

Nikola Tesla is not a name that many people know. When people think of electricity, they often think of people like Thomas Edison or Michael Faraday. Sure, Edison invented the lightbulb and Faraday made huge contributions to electromagnetism, but Tesla was a genius as well. He invented a motor that runs on alternating current, which is one of the two dominant types of electricity (the other is direct current). He also thought up many other inventions, such as:

  • A radio-controlled wireless boat (one of the first wireless devices)
  • A way to create artificial lightning
  • A steam-powered mechanical oscillator
  • A VTOL (Vertical Take-Off and Landing) biplane

Much of his work has been obscured by other inventors and other inventions, but he really made a lot of interesting devices. This webpage aims to show off some of those while giving readers more information about Nikola Tesla.

Early Life

Austrian Empire

Nikola Tesla was born on July 10th, 1856, in what is now Croatia. His father was a priest and his mother managed the family farm. His family moved to Gospić, Austrian Empire, in 1862. He first became interested in electricty in his physics class. One demonstration that particularly interested him was a "radiometer the professor had built out of a bulb and tinfoil" (Wikipedia, “Nikola Tesla”). He stated that he wanted to learn more about electricity.

In 1874, Tesla managed to evade the draft in Smiljan by running away to Tomingaj, a town near Gračac. A year later, he went to Austrian Polytechnic in Graz, Austria, on a scholarship, and in his first year, he "never missed a lecture, earned the highest grades possible, passed nine exams (nearly twice as many as required), [and] started a Serb culture club" (Wikipedia, "Nikola Tesla"). However, in his second year, he lost his scholarship and became addicted to gambling. He gambled his allowance and tuition money away, but later gambled it back. He never graduated from the university.

After that, he jumped around a lot. First, he severed all ties with family and friends so that they wouldn't know he dropped out of school. Then he moved to present-day Slovenia where he worked at a draftsman before being sent back to his hometown of Gospić due to his lack of a residence permit. In 1880, two of Tesla's uncles gathered enough money to help him go to Prague, where he studied. He moved to Budapest in 1881, where he again worked as a draftsman. Finally, in 1882, he moved to France and began working for the Continental Edison Company.

Tesla and Edison

After Tesla moved to France in 1882, he began working for the Continental Edison Company, where he worked with different types of electrical equipment. In June of 1884, he moved to New York City, and he would stay in the U.S.A. for the rest of his life. He was hired by Thomas Edison to work at his Edison Machine Works, where Tesla quickly went from doing simple electrical engineering to solving more complex problems.

Edison asked Tesla to work on completely redesigining the company's DC (direct current) generators. Tesla said he could make huge improvements to them, and Edison said that he "would pay $50,000 for an improved design for his DC dynamos" (History, "Nikola Tesla"). Tesla was easily able to do this, but when he presented his solution to Edison and asked for his payment, Edison said that it was just a joke and offered a $10 raise instead. Tesla refused and immediately resigned from Edison's company.

Tesla's Contributions to Computer Science

Tesla's AC Induction Motor

After he left Edison's company, Tesla made some very important discoveries. Arguably the most important of these was his invention of an induction motor that ran on AC (alternating current), which was a relatively new system of current at the time. His work with AC and his public demonstrations of its features helped popularize it. This also helped in the "War of Currents", in which Edison's DC battled with AC for public attention and usage. AC was important to computer science because it is used widely today. Wall outlets produce alternating current because it is the most efficient way to transport power from power plants to homes. Tesla's motor is used in a variety of industry and houeshold appliances today. However, there's more - "Tesla’s greatest achievement is his polyphase alternating current system, which is today lighting the entire globe" (Tesla Memorial Society, "Tesla Biography"). This shows that Tesla's work still has a huge impact today.

Tesla also worked with X-rays, and some of his early experiments included cold cathode tubes. This type of cathode doesn't need a filament to heat it up. Tesla's discovieries with X-rays were relevant to computer science because cold cathode tubes were used in some vacuum tubes, which were central components of early computers. Tesla's cathode tubes were very new technology at his time. Other people started to make similar tubes a few decades after Tesla - for example, one of my group members wrote about the Williams-Kilburn tube. Another one of my group memebers made a webpage about the first computer - Colossus. This computer used vacuum tubes to function, and Tesla's cold cathode tubes were a predecessor of vacuum tubes.

After his endeavors into X-rays, Tesla worked on radio technology. He created one of the first wirelessly controlled devices - a radio-controlled boat. He demonstrated this to the public at an electrical exhibition, and since they hadn't seen anything like it, they "made outrageous claims about the workings of the boat, such as magic, telepathy, and being piloted by a trained monkey hidden inside" (Wikipedia, "Nikola Tesla"). These innovations in radio are important because they were some of the first wireless inventions. Today, we use wireless networking and connection heavily, so its surprising that it was already being talked about 100 years ago. However, Tesla initially didn't receive the credit he deserved. Instead, a man named Guglielmo Marconi took all the credit for making a working radio system, but Tesla knew that Marconi's work was largely based off of some of Tesla's patented inventions. In 1943, the year of Tesla's death, the U.S. Supreme Court restored Tesla's patents and gave him the recognition he worked for.

Tesla's Later Years

Wardenclyffe Tower

When Tesla finished with his radio work, he began construction on Wardenclyffe Tower, where he would try to work on wireless communication and more advanced radio signals. However, his financial backer, J.P. Morgan, refused to give him more funding after his initial funding ran out. This stopped the tower's construction, and in that time, Marconi transmitted the letter "S" from England to Newfoundland, beating Tesla to be the first person to make such a transmission. Tesla never finished the tower and he was later forced to sell it for a little over 10% of its purchase price.

After Wardenclyffe Tower, much of Tesla's work fell into obscurity, which may have been a good thing. He was working on devices like a "death ray", and a "directed energy weapon", which may not have been the best thing for the world to have - World War II was coming up and nobody would want more death and destruction than there already was. Tesla died on January 7, 1983. He was alone in his room in the New Yorker Hotel and his body was found when his maid came to his room two days after his death. The FBI ordered the Alien Property Custodian to seize Tesla's belongings even though he was an American citizen. They probably wanted to keep things like the death ray out of the hands of the public, but we'll never know for sure.

Tesla was an underappreciated genius who contributed to early and modern computer science. His work was often overshadowed by more well-known names like Thomas Edison, but his contributions will not be forgotten.
